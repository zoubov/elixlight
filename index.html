Scenarios to consider

Lighting strategy becomes particularly impactful in situations where human states are not stable or predictable.

Jet Lag & Travel Recovery

Spaces designed for international travelers often prioritize comfort, but do not support circadian realignment.

→ Lighting can guide the body through re-synchronization with local time.

Evening Environments That Remain Too Stimulating

Residential or hospitality spaces that maintain daytime lighting logic into the evening.

→ Lighting can support downregulation and sleep preparation.

Wellness Spaces with Visual Overstimulation

Environments intended for relaxation that rely on contrast and visual intensity.

→ Lighting can reduce sensory load and support deeper recovery.

Social Spaces with Low Emotional Comfort

Restaurants or lounges where exposure and brightness affect interaction.

→ Lighting can increase perceived safety and ease of connection.

Work + Living Hybrid Spaces

Rooms used for both productivity and rest without clear separation.

→ Lighting can define transitions between activation and decompression.

Applied Lighting Strategy — Sample Project

Applied Lighting Strategy

A conceptual project demonstrating how hormonal and circadian principles can inform lighting design in a luxury hospitality environment.

01 — PROJECT OVERVIEW

Project Overview

Project Type

Luxury Coastal Resort

Project Focus

Rest, recovery and circadian alignment

User Profile

Guests seeking relaxation, disconnection from stress, and improved sleep quality

Project Intent

This sample project explores how lighting can move beyond visual design and actively support physiological regulation.

In hospitality environments, rest is often approached aesthetically. This project reframes rest as a biological process influenced by light.

02 — DESIGN APPROACH

Design Approach

Lighting is treated as a regulatory layer, not only a visual one.

The approach is based on three principles:

  • Light influences hormonal activity
  • Hormonal activity shapes human states
  • Human states define spatial experience

Objective

To align lighting behavior with:

  • activation
  • relaxation
  • social connection
  • sleep preparation
03 — HUMAN STATE GOALS

Human State Goals

The project is structured around four key daily states:

Morning Activation

Supporting wakefulness, alertness and energy

Daytime Balance

Maintaining stable energy and visual comfort

Evening Decompression

Reducing stimulation and preparing the body for rest

Sleep Preparation

Supporting melatonin production and deep rest

04 — HORMONAL INTENT

Hormonal Intent

Lighting is designed to support the following biological responses:

Morning

Target:

  • cortisol activation
  • dopamine engagement

Lighting implications:

  • increasing intensity
  • cooler spectrum
  • directional exposure

Day

Target:

  • stable alertness
  • reduced fatigue

Lighting implications:

  • balanced intensity
  • neutral spectrum
  • visual comfort

Evening

Target:

  • reduced cortisol
  • transition toward melatonin production

Lighting implications:

  • gradual dimming
  • warmer spectrum
  • reduced overhead light

Night

Target:

  • melatonin support
  • minimal disruption

Lighting implications:

  • very low intensity
  • warm, indirect light
  • localized sources only
05 — HUMAN STATE MAPPING

Human State Mapping

Each space is defined by its intended human effect.

Lobby → mild stimulation, orientation, arrival energy

Restaurant → social connection, comfort

Bar → sensory stimulation, engagement

Guest Rooms → restoration, sleep preparation

Spa → deep relaxation, parasympathetic activation

06 — CIRCADIAN LIGHTING FLOW

Circadian Lighting Flow

The lighting experience evolves throughout the day:

Morning

Bright, directional, cooler tones → supports wakefulness

Daytime

Balanced, stable light → supports sustained function

Evening

Gradual reduction in intensity. Warmer tones → supports decompression

Night

Minimal, low-level lighting → supports sleep continuity

07 — LIGHTING BEHAVIORAL PRINCIPLES

Lighting Behavioral Principles

This strategy focuses on behavior, not fixtures.

  • Gradual transitions instead of abrupt lighting changes
  • Avoidance of prolonged overhead lighting in evening environments
  • Use of lateral (eye-level) lighting in social spaces
  • Reduction of glare to support visual comfort and relaxation
  • Differentiation between zones to reinforce daily rhythm
  • Limitation of visual contrast in recovery environments
08 — ZONE-SPECIFIC GUIDELINES

Zone-Specific Guidelines

Guest Room — Evening / Night

Intent: restoration and sleep preparation

Lighting:

  • low intensity
  • warm spectrum
  • indirect sources
  • minimal ceiling exposure

Restaurant

Intent: connection and comfort

Lighting:

  • warm tones
  • eye-level sources
  • soft contrast
  • reduced glare

Spa

Intent: deep relaxation

Lighting:

  • very low intensity
  • minimal contrast
  • indirect light only
  • absence of visual stimulation

Lobby

Intent: arrival and orientation

Lighting:

  • moderate intensity
  • defined focal points
  • balanced contrast
09 — DESIGN REVIEW (EXAMPLE)

Design Review (Example)

This section demonstrates how lighting proposals are evaluated.

Observation

Consistent overhead lighting is used across all evening environments.

Impact

  • prolonged alertness
  • delayed melatonin production
  • reduced ability to unwind

Recommendation

  • introduce layered lighting
  • reduce ceiling dominance
  • shift toward warmer tones and lower intensity

Result

Improved alignment with the intended evening experience and guest recovery.
